

## Definition

**Lazarus's Appraisal Theory of Emotion** (developed by Richard Lazarus) posits that emotions are extracted from our _evaluations_ (appraisals) of events, rather than being direct reactions to the events themselves.

According to this theory, cognition is a necessary precursor to emotion. An individual must first assess a stimulus to determine its personal significance before an emotional response is triggered. This explains why the same event can provoke entirely different emotional responses in different people.

## The Appraisal Process

Lazarus divided the cognitive appraisal process into three continuous and interconnected stages:

### 1. Primary Appraisal (Relevance)

Evaluating the significance of an event in relation to personal goals and well-being.

- **Goal Relevance:** Does this event matter to me? (If no, no emotion is generated).
    
- **Goal Congruence:** Is this event beneficial (positive emotion) or harmful (negative emotion) to my goals?
    
- **Type of Ego-Involvement:** How does this affect my identity, values, or self-esteem?
    

### 2. Secondary Appraisal (Coping)

Evaluating the resources and options available to cope with the event.

- **Blame or Credit:** Who or what is responsible for the event?
    
- **Coping Potential:** Do I have the psychological, physical, or social resources to manage this? (e.g., problem-focused vs. emotion-focused coping).
    
- **Future Expectancy:** Is the situation likely to improve or worsen?
    

### 3. Reappraisal

A continuous feedback loop. As the situation unfolds or new information is acquired, the individual reassesses the event, which can alter or replace the initial emotional response.

## Key Properties and Distinctions

- **Cognitive Primacy:** Directly contrasts with the [[James-Lange Theory]] (which argues physiological arousal precedes emotion) by asserting that thought must precede the emotional response.
    
- **Subjectivity:** It inherently accounts for individual differences in emotional responses to identical stimuli, making it highly robust for modeling human behavior.
    

## Applications in Affective Computing

- **Computational Models of Emotion:** Appraisal theory forms the theoretical foundation for many computational emotion architectures (e.g., the EMA model - Emotion and Adaptation). These models use rules to simulate "Primary" and "Secondary" appraisals to generate realistic emotional states in virtual agents and NPCs.
    
- **Context-Aware AI:** Guides the development of LLMs and AI systems that attempt to infer user emotion not just from text or tone, but by analyzing the user's goals, context, and coping potential (e.g., personalized mental health chatbots).
